Defensive Line Speed: How Elite Teams Are Winning the Gainline Battle

Published: 10 May 2024
Source: The Rugby Coach Weekly

In the modern game, defensive line speed has become the defining battleground at club and international level. Teams like Leinster, Toulouse, and the All Blacks consistently win the gainline battle not through brute force but through coordinated defensive systems that apply pressure before ball carriers can shift the point of attack.

The blitz defence, once considered a high-risk strategy employed only at the elite level, is now being adapted successfully at club level. The key is in the communication between the defensive pillar and the inside centre, who must call the trigger for the blitz before the scrum-half picks up the ball.

Research from URC data analysts found that teams winning more than 65% of gainline contacts were 40% more likely to win the match. This underscores how central the defensive collision is to modern game plans.

Coaching points for club coaches:

1. Start with a slow-walk through the blitz system before adding any pace. Players need to understand the trigger, not just react to it.

2. The inside defender (blindside flanker) must not drift — they hold the 9 channel. Every other defender sets their depth off this anchor point.

3. Use a shadow session with two teams walking through the system against the attack in a grid. The defence calls out their man and the trigger simultaneously.

4. Progress to live sessions only when players can identify and call the blitz trigger from the set piece without coaching prompts.

Practical exercise: Set up a grid 30m x 25m. Attackers have the ball on their 22 with a scrum half and two pods of three. Defenders practise holding depth then driving the line speed from the trigger call. Run 6 reps per set, three sets per session.

Youth application: This system works with U18 and above. For younger age groups, focus first on individual tackle technique and body position before introducing team defensive systems.
